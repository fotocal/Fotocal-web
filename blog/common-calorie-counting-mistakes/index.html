7 Calorie Counting Mistakes That Quietly Stall Progress

If the scale won’t move despite “eating in a deficit”, the deficit probably isn’t as real as your log suggests. Here are the usual culprits.

July 9, 2026·5 min read

Calorie counting works — when it’s done accurately. Most “I’m tracking and still not losing” cases come down to a handful of predictable errors. Fix these and progress usually resumes.

1. Eyeballing portions

Guessing is the number-one error. People underestimate portions by 20–40%, especially calorie-dense foods. A kitchen scale fixes it fast.

2. Forgetting liquid calories

Juice, lattes, smoothies, alcohol and “just a splash” of oil add up quickly and are easy to ignore. Log them like food.

3. Not counting cooking oil

A tablespoon of oil is around 120 calories, and most pans get two or three. Untracked oil alone can erase a deficit.

4. Trusting inaccurate database entries

Crowd-sourced food databases are full of wrong entries. Cross-check against the label and favor verified sources.

5. “Bites, licks and tastes”

The handful of nuts, the kid’s leftover fries, the spoon of peanut butter — a few untracked nibbles a day can total hundreds of calories.

6. Only tracking on weekdays

Five tight days and two loose weekend days can wipe out the weekly deficit. Track every day, including the fun ones.

7. Never updating your target

As you lose weight, maintenance falls. A target set two months ago may no longer create a deficit. Recalculate periodically.

Consistency beats perfection — but only if what you’re consistently logging is actually accurate.

Key takeaways

  • Weigh portions and always count oil and liquids.
  • Verify database entries against labels.
  • Track weekends and small bites too.
