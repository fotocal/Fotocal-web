High-Protein Diet: Benefits and How to Start

If there’s one dietary change with the most upside for the least downside, eating more protein is it.

February 18, 2026·6 min read

Among all the diet strategies out there, simply increasing your protein intake stands out for how consistently it helps — for fat loss, muscle, satiety and body composition — with very little downside for most healthy people. Here’s why, and how to start.

The benefits

  • Keeps you full: protein is the most satiating macro, so you eat less without trying.
  • Protects muscle: in a calorie deficit, adequate protein preserves lean mass.
  • Burns more in digestion: protein has the highest thermic effect — you spend more calories digesting it.
  • Supports recovery and strength: it’s the raw material for building and repairing muscle.

How much protein?

For most people pursuing fat loss or muscle, 1.6–2.2g per kg of bodyweight is a strong target. A simple shortcut is roughly one gram per pound of goal bodyweight. There’s no need to go to extremes beyond this range.

Easy ways to eat more

  • Anchor every meal with a clear protein source.
  • Make breakfast protein-forward (eggs, Greek yogurt, protein oats).
  • Keep high-protein snacks handy: yogurt, cottage cheese, jerky, edamame.
  • Use a whey or plant protein shake to fill gaps.

Is high protein safe?

For healthy people, higher protein intakes in this range are well-tolerated and safe. Those with existing kidney disease should consult a doctor. Drink enough water and include plenty of vegetables and fiber alongside the protein.

Key takeaways

  • More protein aids fullness, muscle, digestion and recovery.
  • Aim for 1.6–2.2g per kg of bodyweight.
  • Anchor meals with protein and keep high-protein snacks ready.
  • It’s safe for healthy people; track to hit your target.
