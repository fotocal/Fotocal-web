How to Track Calories Without Weighing Every Meal

A scale is the gold standard, but it isn’t the only way. With a few reliable visual anchors, you can estimate closely enough to make real progress.

July 3, 2026·5 min read

Weighing food is the most accurate way to track, but it isn’t always practical — restaurants, travel, someone else’s cooking. The good news: you can estimate surprisingly well once you learn a few consistent reference points.

The hand portion method

Your hand travels with you and scales roughly to your body size, which makes it a handy built-in measuring tool:

  • Protein: a palm-sized portion (≈20–30g protein).
  • Carbs: a cupped hand (≈20–30g carbs).
  • Fats: a thumb (≈10g fat).
  • Vegetables: a full fist or more.

Useful visual anchors

Memorize a few everyday comparisons: a deck of cards is about 85g of meat, a tennis ball is roughly a cup, a poker chip is a tablespoon, and a golf ball is about two tablespoons. These let you eyeball a plate in seconds.

Calibrate occasionally

Even without daily weighing, weigh your common foods once or twice to check how good your eye is. Most people find their estimates drift low, so a periodic reality check keeps you honest.

Aim for consistent, not perfect

The goal of no-scale tracking isn’t precision to the gram. It’s a stable, repeatable estimate you can trust week to week. If your weight moves in the right direction, your estimates are good enough.

Key takeaways

  • Use hand portions: palm, cupped hand, thumb, fist.
  • Memorize visual anchors like a deck of cards or tennis ball.
  • Calibrate your eye by weighing common foods occasionally.
  • Photo logging removes guesswork for un-weighable meals.
