Weight Loss Plateaus: Why They Happen and How to Break Them

A plateau isn’t a sign your body is broken. It’s a sign the math has changed — and math is fixable.

May 28, 2026·6 min read

You were losing steadily, then the scale froze for two, three, four weeks. Frustrating, but almost always explainable. Here’s what’s really going on and how to restart progress without panicking.

First, is it actually a plateau?

A few flat days are just water noise. A true plateau is three-plus weeks with no change in your weekly average. If it’s been less than that, keep going — you may not have plateaued at all.

Reason 1: Your maintenance dropped

A lighter body burns fewer calories. The deficit that once worked has shrunk as you lost weight. This is the most common and most legitimate cause.

Reason 2: Tracking drifted

Portions creep up, bites go unlogged, and estimates slide over time. Re-tighten your tracking for a week — weigh foods again — and the deficit often reappears on its own.

Reason 3: Less daily movement

Diets can unconsciously make you move less (NEAT drops) — fewer fidgets, slower walking. Your workouts may be intact while your background activity quietly fell.

How to break it

  • Recalculate your calories for your new weight and trim 100–200 if needed.
  • Tighten tracking for a week to check for hidden creep.
  • Add movement — a daily step goal is the easiest lever.
  • Consider a diet break at maintenance for a week or two to reset adherence.

Key takeaways

  • A real plateau is 3+ weeks of no average change.
  • Usual causes: lower maintenance, loose tracking, less movement.
  • Recalculate calories, tighten logging, add steps.
  • A short diet break can restore adherence.
